⚡ Power & Energy Profile

Enterprise facilities at this scale typically run evaporative cooling to achieve better PUE, but at the cost of significant water consumption. Total facility draw at PUE 1.4 = ~84 MW, or approximately 735,840 MWh/year.

🔴 Grid impact: An 84 MW dedicated feed is a transformative load for rural Maine distribution. A new 115kV substation and transmission line upgrades would almost certainly be required. ISO-NE interconnection costs at this scale routinely exceed $50M and take 5–8 years to complete.

💧 Water Use — Critical Concern

Evaporative cooling at this scale consumes approximately 50 million gallons per year — the equivalent of the annual water use of roughly 450 average Maine households. This is where the enterprise DC scenario diverges sharply from the edge tier.

⚖️ Environmental Considerations

⚠️ Water Withdrawal Permitting

Maine DEP Water Withdrawal Permit required for any withdrawal >10,000 gpd from surface or groundwater. At 50M gal/yr (~137,000 gpd), this proposal triggers full DEP review including public comment and potential Great Ponds Act analysis.

🔴 Thermal Discharge

Cooling tower blowdown and thermal discharge into municipal wastewater creates heat loading in receiving waters. Maine DEP Chapter 581 water temperature standards apply. May require dedicated discharge treatment.

📌 Impervious Surface & Stormwater

30-acre site: typical build covers ~20 acres with impervious surface (buildings, parking, generator pads). Stormwater detention required. Potential wetland impact depending on parcel — Sec. 404 Army Corps review if wetlands present.

⚡ Backup Generation — Air Quality

Large facilities require multi-MW diesel backup generator capacity. Regular load testing (monthly) generates NOx and PM2.5. Maine DEP Air Emission License required above 200 HP aggregate. Proximity to residences matters significantly.

💵 Fiscal Reality Check

What the Community Gets

  • ~$2,000,000/yr estimated property tax est.
  • 80 FTE year-round operations jobs est.
  • ~500 construction jobs (24–36 months, temporary)
  • $350M one-time construction activity
  • Fiber and power infrastructure that may serve broader community

What the Community Provides

  • New 115kV substation + transmission upgrades (~$50M+)
  • 50M gal/yr continuous water commitment (year-round)
  • Wastewater treatment capacity for thermal blowdown
  • Road reinforcement for heavy construction traffic (multi-year)
  • Emergency services (hazmat-rated, 24/7)
  • High risk: Tax stabilization agreements (TIF/BETR) common in Maine — could defer real revenue 20+ years
🔴 TIF/BETR warning: In Maine, data centers routinely negotiate Tax Increment Financing (TIF) or Business Equipment Tax Reimbursement (BETR) agreements that significantly reduce or defer property and equipment tax obligations. The headline $2M/yr tax figure may not materialize for 10–20 years. Always demand the net present value calculation before any vote.
Tax estimate: York County mil rate applied to $200M assessed value. TIF/BETR risk: Maine Revenue Services BETR program data; Portland Press Herald coverage of Westbrook DC negotiations 2025–2026. Full methodology: methodology.md.
