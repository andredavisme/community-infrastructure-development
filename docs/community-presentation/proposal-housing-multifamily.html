🏠 Scale & Infrastructure Impact

At 300 units on 25 acres (12 units/acre), this is the highest-density scenario in the housing set. It requires multi-story construction, structured parking, and significant utility infrastructure — all of which are uncommon in rural York County but increasingly necessary to address housing demand.

📌 Sewer Capacity: Critical

~100,000+ gpd of continuous wastewater is a major load on any rural municipal system. Developer must fund a capacity study and any required system upgrades before permit issuance. Phased occupancy tied to sewer confirmation recommended.

📌 Road & Traffic Impact

300 units generating 1.0–1.5 vehicle trips per unit per peak hour = 300–450 additional peak-hour trips. Traffic impact study required. Road improvements at primary access points likely required at developer expense.

✅ Density Efficiency

Multi-family is more infrastructure-efficient per unit than single-family: lower water use per capita (shared laundry, smaller units), lower road miles per unit, lower per-unit emergency service cost. Per-unit municipal cost of services is lower than subdivision.

📌 Character & Zoning

300-unit multi-family is likely to require a zoning variance or PUD approval in most York County coastal communities where current zoning favors low-density residential. Community process must precede any permit application.

⚖️ Environmental Considerations

✅ Chemical Use: LOW

No agricultural or industrial chemical program. Shared landscape maintenance is professionally managed but low-input. No chemical risk comparable to golf course or data center cooling systems.

📌 Impervious Surface

Multi-story construction on 25 acres: impervious surface ~15–18 acres (buildings, parking, drives). Maine DEP Chapter 500 stormwater management required. Green roof, permeable paving, and bioretention should be design requirements.

💧 Cumulative Water Impact

41M gal/yr (~6.3% of KKWD annual supply) is a measurable fractional draw. Added to existing seasonal demand curve, this development materially affects system headroom. Independent water capacity analysis required before any approval.

✅ Reduced Per-Capita Footprint

Multi-family housing has the lowest land consumption per resident of any housing type. 300 units on 25 acres vs. 300 single-family lots requiring ~240+ acres. Concentration reduces total habitat fragmentation at comparable population growth.

💵 Fiscal Reality Check

What the Community Gets

  • ~$1.1M/yr estimated property tax est.
  • ~750 new residents (year-round, est.)
  • $90M one-time construction activity
  • ~300–400 construction jobs (24–36 months)
  • Rental housing supply addressing market shortage

What the Community Provides

  • Water & wastewater capacity (~112K gpd year-round)
  • School services (enrollment impact)
  • Road infrastructure and traffic mitigation
  • Emergency services at increased call volume
  • Zoning and permitting process (PUD likely required)
⚠️ Key question: At $1.1M/yr in tax revenue, this is the strongest fiscal performer of the three housing scenarios — but it also places the largest demands on water, wastewater, roads, and schools. The fiscal case depends on whether infrastructure upgrade costs are borne by the developer or deferred to residents through bonding. Require developer-funded infrastructure reports as a condition of pre-application.
