🏠 Housing Mix & Community Need

York County has a documented workforce housing shortage driven by seasonal wage employment, high property values, and limited rental stock. A 30% affordable set-aside addresses a genuine community need that market-rate development alone does not fill.

✅ Workforce Housing Supply

45 affordable/workforce units directly address the housing gap for hospitality, healthcare, and municipal workers who cannot afford coastal Maine market rents. Maine HAC documents median rent burden exceeding 35% of income for many York County renters.

✅ Year-Round Population Stability

Mixed-income developments support year-round community viability: children in schools, local spending, and civic participation. Coastal communities dependent on seasonal workers benefit from permanent local housing stock.

📌 School & Services Demand

150 units with year-round occupancy will generate meaningful school enrollment demand. Affordable units with families skew younger household composition. School cost impact must be modeled separately from tax revenue.

📌 Affordable Unit Tax Reduction

Deed-restricted affordable units are assessed at below-market values, reducing per-unit tax contribution. LIHTC-financed units may carry additional valuation complications. Net tax yield is lower than 150 fully market-rate units.

⚖️ Environmental Considerations

✅ Chemical Use: LOW

No commercial chemical program. Shared landscape maintenance (common areas) is modest in scale. No industrial or agricultural inputs.

📌 Density & Stormwater

20 acres at 150 units (7.5 units/acre) is moderate density. Impervious surface ~10–12 acres including parking and paths. Maine DEP Chapter 500 stormwater management required. Green infrastructure (rain gardens, permeable paving) should be required in approval conditions.

💧 Sewer Capacity

At ~56,250 gpd average and year-round occupancy, wastewater load is continuous and substantial. Municipal sewer capacity must be verified before any unit is occupied. Phased build-out may help manage system load.

✅ Infill Preference

Siting within or adjacent to existing developed areas reduces habitat fragmentation, infrastructure extension costs, and road miles compared to greenfield subdivision. Infill sites should be prioritized in site selection.

💵 Fiscal Reality Check

What the Community Gets

  • ~$600,000/yr estimated blended property tax est.
  • ~375 new residents (year-round, est.)
  • 45 affordable units addressing documented housing need
  • $45M one-time construction activity
  • ~150–200 construction jobs (18–24 months)

What the Community Provides

  • School services (year-round enrollment impact)
  • Water & wastewater capacity (~56K gpd year-round)
  • Road maintenance and public transit access
  • Emergency services (fire, EMS, police)
  • Potential LIHTC compliance monitoring obligations
💡 Key question: Mixed-income development is the scenario most likely to produce genuine long-term community benefit — it addresses a real need, stabilizes workforce, and generates year-round tax base. The fiscal test is whether school and services cost exceeds the blended tax yield. In Maine, that balance depends heavily on household composition and school enrollment.
