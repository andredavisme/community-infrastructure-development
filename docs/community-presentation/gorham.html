Cumberland County — Municipal

Gorham

A fast-growing suburban town southwest of Portland, home to the University of Southern Maine’s main campus. Gorham has seen significant residential expansion driven by its proximity to Greater Portland and relatively lower land costs.

💧 Water & Wastewater Capacity

Gorham is served by the Portland Water District (PWD) for public water supply in its village and growth areas. Wastewater is managed through a mix of municipal sewer and on-site septic depending on location within town.

📌 Water Supply — PWD

Portland Water District extends service into Gorham’s developed areas via the Sebago Lake system. Rural and outlying parcels may rely on private wells — capacity is parcel-specific in those zones.

🚽 Wastewater — Mixed System

The Gorham village center and newer subdivisions connect to municipal sewer. Significant portions of town remain on septic, which creates site-by-site capacity constraints for higher-density proposals outside the sewer service area.

⚖️ Environmental & Land Context

Gorham’s landscape is a mix of active farmland, forested parcels, wetland corridors, and expanding suburban development. The Presumpscot River forms part of Gorham’s northern boundary with Westbrook and Windham.

🌿 Wetland Corridors

Gorham contains numerous mapped wetlands, particularly in its western and northern sectors. Maine’s Natural Resources Protection Act applies to any work that alters or fills jurisdictional wetland. Mitigation is required; avoidance is preferred.

💧 Presumpscot River Buffer

The Presumpscot River along Gorham’s northern edge is subject to Shoreland Zoning. The 250 ft buffer applies to development near the river corridor and associated floodplain areas.

🌿 Agricultural Land

Gorham retains active farmland under the Maine Farmland Trust and voluntary conservation easements. While not a hard regulatory constraint, preserved farmland shapes the development envelope in rural zones.

🏗 Growth & Zoning

Gorham has a defined growth boundary and active comprehensive planning process. The town has approved several large residential subdivisions in recent years, with ongoing debate around infrastructure capacity to support continued growth.
