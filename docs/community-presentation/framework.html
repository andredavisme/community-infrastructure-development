Responsible Decision-Making Framework

A community’s job is not to accept or reject development — it’s to ask the right questions, demand honest answers, and protect what residents actually value. This framework applies to any infrastructure proposal, anywhere.

The Core Principle

“A good infrastructure decision improves the community’s long-term capacity — it does not consume it.”

This means the right question is never just “does this project create jobs?” or “does it generate tax revenue?” The right question is: after this project is built and operating for 20 years, is the community better positioned than it was before?

💧 Dimension 1: Utility & Infrastructure Capacity

✅
Quantify total demand at peak, not average. Golf course irrigation, housing occupancy, commercial traffic — all peak seasonally. Capacity analysis must use peak-demand figures, not annual averages.
✅
Account for overlapping peaks. If the community’s population triples in summer, any summer-intensive use competes with existing peak demand. Both peaks must fit within capacity simultaneously.
✅
Identify who pays for capacity upgrades. If the project requires expanded water lines, road widening, or wastewater treatment upgrades, the developer — not residents — should bear those costs.
🔴
Require a binding water use agreement. Benchmark numbers are estimates. A binding agreement caps actual water use and defines consequences if the cap is exceeded.

⚖️ Dimension 2: Environmental Integrity

✅
Require a full wetland delineation before any vote. Don’t accept developer-produced maps without independent verification. Wetland boundaries determine regulatory exposure and mitigation cost.
✅
Map all wellhead protection zones on the site. Any pesticide or fertilizer program must demonstrate how it avoids contaminating public water supply areas.
✅
Obtain all DEP permits before construction begins. NRPA permits are not guaranteed. Conditional approvals that assume permits will be issued create stranded investment risk for the community.
🔴
Require a 10-year chemical use monitoring plan. Pesticide and fertilizer impacts on groundwater and coastal waters are cumulative. A monitoring plan with enforceable thresholds is the only meaningful protection.

💵 Dimension 3: Fiscal Responsibility

✅
Require a 20-year fiscal impact analysis, not just year-one projections. Infrastructure ages. Operating costs grow. Tax exemptions expire or don’t. The community needs the long view.
✅
Distinguish seasonal jobs from year-round jobs. A project that creates 35 summer jobs and 8 year-round jobs has a fundamentally different fiscal profile than 35 FTE year-round positions.
✅
Account for BETE exposure. Business equipment is often exempt from property tax under Maine’s BETE program. Large equipment-intensive operations (maintenance fleets, irrigation systems) may reduce actual tax yield below projections.
🔴
Include a Community Benefit Agreement (CBA). If the project benefits from public infrastructure, it should contribute to the community proportionally: affordable memberships, local hiring requirements, or direct municipal contributions.

👥 Dimension 4: Community Voice

✅
Hold public meetings before any permit application is filed. Community input at the design stage can shape a better project. Input after permits are filed is mostly symbolic.
✅
Publish all materials in plain language with adequate notice. Technical reports filed 48 hours before a vote are not meaningful public participation. Require 30-day public comment periods on major filings.
🔴
Document every question raised and every answer given. If a developer commits to something verbally at a public meeting, that commitment should be in writing before the vote.

The Value-Add Test

A proposal clears the bar as a genuine value-add only when it satisfies all five of the following:

  1. Fits within capacity at peak demand, with margin to spare — not just on paper, but accounting for seasonal overlap.
  2. Leaves the environment better, or at worst neutral — wetlands intact, water quality protected, chemical plan enforceable.
  3. Pays its own way over 20 years — tax revenue exceeds the cost of services it consumes, without requiring exemptions or subsidies.
  4. Creates durable community benefit — year-round jobs, public access, affordable amenities, or direct municipal contributions, not just seasonal activity and private profit.
  5. Earned community consent through genuine engagement — residents understood the trade-offs and supported the project with full information, not marketing.
